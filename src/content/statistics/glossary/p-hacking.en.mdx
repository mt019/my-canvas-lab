## What it says

p-hacking is adjusting the analysis while watching the result: collect twenty more subjects and look again, drop the covariate that misbehaves, switch to another outcome measure, change the rule for excluding outliers. Each step has a defensible reason. Together they amount to using the data to choose the analysis, and then presenting the analysis as if it had been fixed in advance.

Malice is not required. Most p-hacking happens in the sincere course of cleaning up a dataset.

## One worked example

<Cite id="simmonsNelsonSimonsohn2011FalsePositive">Simmons, Nelson and Simonsohn simply did it on purpose in 2011</Cite>: subjects listened either to the Beatles' "When I'm Sixty-Four" or to a control song, then reported their date of birth. The finding was that the song made them a year and a half younger (20.1 years against 21.5, $F(1,17) = 4.92$, $p = .040$).

Music does not reverse ageing. The point was to show that a little undeclared flexibility — in how many subjects to run, which covariates to include, which measures to report — can make anything significant. They calculated that this flexibility can drive the false-positive rate from 5% to about 60%. They also noted that their own result collapses to $p = .33$ once a single covariate is removed. That is the demonstration, not a caveat.

## What stops it

Preregistration: commit to the hypothesis, the sample size and the analysis before seeing the data. Exploratory analysis is still welcome — but it has to be labelled exploratory, and its p values cannot be presented as if they still bought a 5% error rate.
