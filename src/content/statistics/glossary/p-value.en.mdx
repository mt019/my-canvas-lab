## What it says

The p value answers one narrow question: **supposing the null were true**, how probable is a result at least as extreme as the one I got? It is a conditional probability, and the condition sits on the null side.

It does not tell you the probability that the null is true. The two run in opposite directions, and thoroughly so: from "if she is guessing, a perfect score has probability 1.4%" you cannot get to "there is a 1.4% chance she is guessing."

## One worked example

There are 70 ways to choose four cups out of eight. Guessing, exactly one of them is the perfect pick, so

$$p = \frac{1}{70} \approx 0.014$$

That 0.014 is the p value. To compute it you first have to lay out everything guessing could have done — which is the <Term id="sampling-distribution">sampling distribution</Term>.

## The usual misreadings

<Cite id="greenland2016">Greenland and colleagues catalogued twenty-five of them in 2016</Cite>. The three most stubborn: p is the probability the null is true (it is not); p = 0.05 means there is a 5% chance the finding is a fluke (it does not); a large p means the null holds (it does not — it means you did not overturn it).

Nor does the size of p tell you the size of the effect. <Cite id="steeringCommittee1988PhysiciansHealthStudy">The aspirin trial on 22,071 physicians reported $p < 0.00001$</Cite>, <Cite id="rosenthal1990SoftPsychology">and an effect size of roughly r = 0.03</Cite>. With a large enough sample, any tiny difference will push p down.
