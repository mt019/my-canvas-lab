## What it says

The null hypothesis is the one you intend to attack with data. It usually says that nothing happened: the two groups are alike, the drug does nothing, she is only guessing. You set it up not because you believe it but because it is specific enough to compute with — specific enough to say what the data should look like if it held, so that real data have something to contradict.

The name misleads in English too. <Cite id="gigerenzerKraussVitouch2004NullEtymology">Null here is closer to nullifiable than to nothing</Cite>. What matters is that it can be overturned.

## One worked example

<Cite id="fisher1935DesignOfExperiments">Fisher's eight cups: four poured milk-first, four tea-first, arranged at random, and Muriel Bristol asked to pick out the four milk-first ones</Cite>. The null is that she cannot tell and is guessing. That sentence is concrete enough to compute: guessing, she gets all four right with probability 1 in 70. The whole point of the experiment is to give the sentence one chance of being overturned.

## What it never gives you

A null hypothesis is never proved. <Cite id="fisher1935DesignOfExperiments">Fisher wrote it plainly: the null hypothesis is never proved or established, but is possibly disproved</Cite>. Failing to overturn it means only that you failed this time — because it is true, or because your sample was small, your measurement crude, your <Term id="statistical-power">power</Term> low. On paper those two situations look identical.
