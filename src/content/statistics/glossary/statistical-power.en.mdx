## What it says

Power is the probability that a study will detect an effect **given that the effect is really there** and is of some stated size. It depends on three things at once: how large the effect is, how large the sample is, and how strict the threshold is.

Power is a quantity you plan with, not one you defend yourself with afterwards. "Observed power," computed after the fact from your own result, is just the p value wearing a different hat.

## One worked example

<Cite id="cohen1962PowerReview">In 1962 Cohen took an entire volume of the Journal of Abnormal and Social Psychology — all 78 articles from 1960 — and worked out the power of every study in it</Cite>. Of the 70 carrying major tests, the average power to detect a medium effect was .48. Slightly worse than a coin toss. For a small effect it was .18.

Which means: even if every one of those hypotheses had been true, half the studies were built to fail. The arithmetic was on the table in 1962. It took the <Term id="replication-crisis">replication crisis</Term>, sixty years later, for the field to read it.

## Why it matters beyond misses

Low power is not only about missing things. In a field where power is generally low, the significant results that do get published are systematically inflated — only the luckiest samples, the ones where noise happened to help, cleared the threshold. That line runs straight into <Term id="positive-predictive-value">positive predictive value</Term>: <Cite id="ioannidis2005WhyMostPublished">half of Ioannidis's 2005 argument rests on low power</Cite>.
