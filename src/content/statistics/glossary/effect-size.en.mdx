## What it says

Effect size measures how big the difference is, on a scale that does not stretch with the sample. The most familiar ruler is Cohen's $d$: the gap between two means, divided by their common standard deviation.

$$d = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}{s}$$

A p value shrinks as the sample grows; an effect size does not. So a paper that reports only p has told you that the difference is unlikely to be coincidence, and nothing at all about whether the difference is worth caring about.

## One worked example

<Cite id="cohen1988PowerAnalysis">Cohen called $d = .2$, $.5$ and $.8$ small, medium and large</Cite>, and anchored each to something you can picture: a small effect is about the mean IQ gap between twins and non-twins; a medium one is about the mean height difference between 14- and 18-year-old girls, "large enough to be visible to the naked eye"; a large one is about the IQ gap between PhD holders and college freshmen.

In the same section Cohen calls the mapping arbitrary. Worth remembering: small, medium and large are a coarse vocabulary for talking to each other, not lines drawn in nature.

## The expensive misreading

"p is tiny, so the effect is big." <Cite id="steeringCommittee1988PhysiciansHealthStudy">The 1988 aspirin trial: 22,071 physicians, 104 heart attacks on aspirin against 189 on placebo, $p < 0.00001$</Cite> — <Cite id="rosenthal1990SoftPsychology">and an effect size around r = 0.03</Cite>. Two numbers from one dataset: one looks like proof, the other is nearly invisible. (That invisible effect saved lives. Small does not mean unimportant — it depends on the cost and the scale.)
