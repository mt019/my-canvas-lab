## What it says

Two ways to be wrong, pointing opposite ways:

- **Type I error** (false alarm): the null is true and you reject it. You announce an effect that is not there. Its long-run rate is what the <Term id="significance-level">significance level</Term> controls.
- **Type II error** (miss): the null is false and you fail to reject. The effect is there and you walked past it. One minus its rate is <Term id="statistical-power">power</Term>.

They trade off. Tighten the threshold and false alarms fall while misses rise. The only way to push both down is to buy more information: a larger sample, or better measurement.

## One worked example

<Cite id="gigerenzer2004MindlessStatistics">Gigerenzer explains the logic with a factory</Cite>: a line making metal plates for surgical use, where machines fail and inspection costs money. A false alarm idles the line for nothing. A miss sends a flawed plate into an operating theatre. The costs are not symmetric, so the manager sets the false-alarm rate very low in advance, tolerates a higher miss rate, and works out from that how many plates to inspect a day.

Nobody in that scene is asking how strong the evidence is. They are asking what this rule will cost them over a year of Mondays. That is <Cite id="neymanPearson1933">what Neyman and Pearson meant by a rule of behaviour</Cite>.

## Why one of them goes missing

When a paper reports $p < .05$, the Type I rate is written down. The Type II rate is usually neither computed nor reported. A whole field can therefore be strict about false alarms while knowing nothing at all about how often it misses.
