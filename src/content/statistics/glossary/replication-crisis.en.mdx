## What it says

The replication crisis is the finding that a large share of published significant results do not reappear when another team runs the same study again. It is an empirical fact and a structural problem: no individual researcher has to break a rule for the system to keep producing it.

## One worked example

<Cite id="openScienceCollaboration2015Reproducibility">In 2015 the Open Science Collaboration, 270 researchers, redid 100 psychology experiments: 97% of the original studies were significant, and 36% of the replications were</Cite>. The replicated effects were, on average, about half the size of the originals.

## How it grows

Three lines meet here, and each can be followed on its own.

Journals prefer significant results, so the null findings stay in the drawer and nobody ever sees them (publication bias). <Term id="statistical-power">Power</Term> is generally low, so the samples that clear the threshold are the lucky ones and the published effect sizes are inflated. Analytic flexibility is everywhere, so <Term id="p-hacking">p-hacking</Term> happens without anyone intending it.

Multiply the three and <Term id="positive-predictive-value">positive predictive value</Term> falls to uncomfortable levels — which is exactly the number Ioannidis computed in 2005, at a time when nobody treated it as a testable prediction. The 2015 replication report is what testing it looked like.
