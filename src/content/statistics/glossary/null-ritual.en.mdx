## What it says

The null ritual is Gigerenzer's name for the statistics of the textbooks: Fisher's evidential logic welded to <Term id="type-i-and-type-ii-error">Neyman and Pearson's</Term> decision logic, paying neither one's price and claiming both authorities.

The routine goes like this. Set up a <Term id="null-hypothesis">null hypothesis</Term> of no difference (never mind what the theory actually predicts). Use $\alpha = .05$ as the threshold (never mind what an error would cost in this particular study). Report $p < .05$ and declare the effect real (while reading the size of p as strength of evidence). Never compute <Term id="statistical-power">power</Term>. Never state the alternative.

## Why it is a weld

Fisher's p value is a scale of evidence, and the price is giving up any guarantee about long-run error rates. Neyman and Pearson's threshold is a rule of behaviour, and the price is fixing $\alpha$ beforehand, naming the alternative, and **not** reading p as evidence. The two rest on incompatible premises.

The textbooks fused them into a third thing that nobody designed. <Cite id="gigerenzerKraussVitouch2004NullRitual">Gigerenzer's point is that the ritual has no author: no statistician will defend the combination, and the field performs it daily</Cite>. <Cite id="gigerenzer2004MindlessStatistics">He calls the resulting practice mindless statistics</Cite> — not because the people doing it are unintelligent, but because the routine was never built to require that you think about what you are doing.

## The way out

<Cite id="wassersteinLazar2016">In 2016 the American Statistical Association did something it had never done before: it issued a formal statement about a statistical method</Cite>. The bluntest of its six principles is that scientific conclusions and policy decisions should not be based only on whether a p value passes a threshold. It offers no list of replacements, because the problem was never the tool. It was the ritual.
